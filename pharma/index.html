The Problem

The reality of evidence generation in pharma

A single systematic literature review takes an average of 1.72 years and costs approximately $141,000 (Borah et al., 2017). The ten largest pharma companies each publish around 119 per year. But for every SLR you commission, there are dozens of internal evidence questions — portfolio decisions, BD&L due diligence, medical affairs queries — that never justify the spend. The evidence base decays the day after completion, and every month spent waiting for evidence is a month patients wait for access decisions.

What if you could run a full systematic review overnight, at a fraction of the cost — and get evidence to decision-makers while it still matters?

Key Finding

Guselkumab Demonstrates Sustained Superiority Over Adalimumab and Ustekinumab

Guselkumab consistently outperforms adalimumab (TNF-α) and ustekinumab (IL-12/23) across induction and maintenance phases, with durable responses through 5 years.

Patients receiving guselkumab had significantly higher PASI 90 rates than ustekinumab at week 16 (70.4% vs. 46.0%, OR 2.79) and week 40 (74.2% vs. 54.5%, OR 2.40) (Diels et al., 2019). Five-year maintenance data confirmed high efficacy levels through week 252, with PASI 90 rates of 76.8–80.6% (Reich et al., 2020).

Forest plot: PASI 90 response rates across 33 studies. REML random-effects pooling. Automatically generated from extracted quantitative outcomes.
Key Insight

The Crossover Effect: IL-23 Inhibitors Surpass IL-17 at Maintenance

IL-17 inhibitors (ixekizumab, bimekizumab) achieve the fastest onset of PASI 90 response at approximately 6–8 weeks (Papp et al., 2025). But by week 48–52, IL-23 inhibitors close and then reverse the gap: risankizumab PASI 90 rises to 86.6% while secukinumab declines to 57.1% (Warren et al., 2020).

At 5 years, 80% of IL-23 patients maintain PASI 90 compared to 65% for IL-17 and 38% for TNF inhibitors. This pattern — IL-17 for rapid control, IL-23 for long-term maintenance — has direct implications for treatment sequencing strategies.

What separates this from deep research tools

  • Every claim grounded to a verbatim quote. Not just a citation — the actual text from the source paper, with section location. Sentences are indexed by number so the model references them by position, not by echoing — it cannot fabricate quotes. If a claim isn't traceable to source text, it doesn't make it into the report.
  • Structured data extraction, not summarisation. Effect sizes, confidence intervals, sample sizes, response rates — extracted into structured schemas, not prose. This is what enables real quantitative pooling rather than a model guessing at a synthesis.
  • Real statistical meta-analysis. REML random-effects pooling, heterogeneity diagnostics (I²/τ²), forest plots, funnel plots, prediction intervals. The same methods a biostatistician would use. The model interprets the results — it doesn't compute them.
  • 20 independent agents, each with one job. Discovery doesn't interpret. Extraction doesn't evaluate. Credibility assessment doesn't know what the claims say. This separation prevents the reasoning shortcuts that monolithic approaches make — where a model that's summarising is also quietly deciding what matters.
  • Multi-dimensional credibility scoring. Every paper scored on randomisation quality, outcome reporting completeness, heterogeneity handling, and funding/conflict of interest — with explicit penalty rules. Not a single “quality: high/medium/low” label.
